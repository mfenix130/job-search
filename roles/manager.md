# Role Overlay: Engineering Manager / Technical Manager

Supplemental signals and theater patterns for engineering management and technical leadership roles.
Includes: Engineering Manager, Senior EM, Director of Engineering, VP Engineering, Tech Lead Manager.
Load this file alongside the resume-reviewer or resume-tailor skill when the role category is Engineering Manager.

---

## What Strong EM Resumes Actually Show

- **Team scope:** how many direct reports, org size, team structure — stated honestly
- **Delivery track record:** what shipped, at what cadence, with what quality bar
- **Talent outcomes:** who grew, who was promoted, who was performance-managed — evidence of real development, not just "mentored engineers"
- **Technical judgment:** decisions the candidate made that had engineering consequences — architecture choices, technology bets, build vs. buy, tech debt prioritization
- **Cross-functional work:** how the candidate operated with product, design, data, sales, legal — who they influenced and how
- **Incident and quality ownership:** how they responded to outages, production failures, systemic quality issues
- **Hiring:** pipeline ownership, bar-raising, sourcing strategy — not just "participated in interviews"
- **Strategy and roadmap:** how they influenced the roadmap vs. received it, what they pushed back on and why
- **Business context:** do they understand the P&L, the revenue implications, the customer impact of their team's work?

## What Weak EM Resumes Do

- List outputs (features shipped, team size) without showing how those outcomes happened
- Claim "grew the team from X to Y" without showing what that hiring looked like or what quality bar they held
- Use "drove alignment" and "built consensus" without showing what was actually decided and how
- Describe every project as successful without any acknowledgment of tradeoffs or failures
- Claim technical depth they no longer exercise ("hands-on manager" when they haven't coded in 3 years)
- Overstate scope ("managed 40 engineers" when they managed 4 who managed 40)
- Use leadership buzzwords that substitute for substance: "servant leadership", "empowered teams", "fostered innovation"

## Theater Patterns to Aggressively Flag

| Phrase | What to probe |
|--------|--------------|
| "Led team delivering X feature" | What was your contribution vs. the team's? What decisions did you make that a senior IC couldn't have? |
| "Grew team from 3 to 12 engineers" | What was the hiring process? What's your hit rate? How many hires have you managed out? |
| "Mentored and developed engineers" | Who got promoted? What specifically did you do that accelerated their growth? |
| "Drove cross-functional alignment" | On what? Who disagreed and why? What did you give up to get agreement? |
| "Improved team velocity by X%" | How was velocity measured? What changed to produce it? Was quality maintained? |
| "Built high-performing engineering culture" | What specifically changed? What did you stop doing? What did you start? |
| "Partnered with product and design" | Who drove the roadmap? What did you push back on and what happened? |
| "Managed $X budget" | What tradeoffs did you make? What did you cut? What did you fight to protect? |

## Seniority Calibration

**Engineering Manager (first-line):**
- Manages 4–10 engineers directly, possibly a team lead or two
- Owns delivery for a team or feature area
- Makes hiring decisions within headcount approved by others
- Should show: team delivery, individual development, process improvement, technical context

**Senior Engineering Manager / Group EM:**
- Manages multiple teams or managers, 15–40 person org
- Owns a product area's engineering strategy, not just a team's delivery
- Has real P&L or headcount budget responsibility
- Should show: org design decisions, manager development, cross-org influence, roadmap ownership

**Director of Engineering:**
- Owns an engineering domain for a product or business unit
- Operates at the business strategy level — influences product direction, defines engineering investment areas
- External hiring bar, partnership with VP/C-suite
- Should show: org structure decisions, engineering strategy with business rationale, VP-level relationships

**VP Engineering:**
- Owns all engineering for a product line or the whole company (at smaller scale)
- Sets engineering culture, bar, process, tooling at the organizational level
- Board/investor visibility, executive team peer
- Should show: company-level decisions, hiring philosophy, engineering brand, retention strategy

## Key Differentiator

The gap between a strong IC lead and a strong EM is not technical depth — it's whether they make the people around them more effective. Probe for evidence that the candidate's presence on a team produced measurably better outcomes than what an equivalent IC + typical management overhead would have produced.
